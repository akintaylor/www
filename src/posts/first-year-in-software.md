title: What my first year in software taught me
date: 2024-11-08

---

I almost quit three times in my first year.

Not because I wasn't making progress. Looking back, I was learning faster than I'd ever learned anything. But I couldn't see it at the time. All I could see was how much everyone around me seemed to already know.

That gap — between where you are and where you think you should be — is the thing nobody prepares you for.

## Imposter syndrome is real, but it's also a trap

Everyone talks about imposter syndrome. It's become almost a meme at this point — "we all feel like we don't belong, haha." But what nobody tells you is that it can become a *reason not to try*.

If you believe you don't deserve to be in the room, you stop asking questions. You stop raising your hand. You start doing the work of making yourself invisible, so that nobody notices you shouldn't be there.

That's the actual damage. Not the feeling — the behavior it produces.

## The thing that actually helped

Mentorship. Specifically, one senior engineer who treated my questions like they were worth answering.

That sounds simple, but it's rarer than it should be. A lot of senior engineers will answer your question, but you can feel the impatience underneath. Like you've interrupted something important.

Find someone who doesn't make you feel that way. And when you're the senior person, be that someone.

## On shipping things that aren't perfect

The biggest mindset shift: realizing that "done" is almost always better than "perfect."

I spent months on a side project that I never shipped because I kept finding things to fix. The project is still sitting in a private GitHub repo. Nobody has ever used it.

Meanwhile, a scrappy little CLI tool I built in a weekend and pushed to npm in a fit of "good enough" has 200 stars.

Ship the thing. Iterate. The feedback loop with real users is worth more than any polish you can do in isolation.
